## Research Report: Electric Vehicles in India

### 1. Executive Summary

The Electric Vehicle (EV) market in India is undergoing a significant transformation, driven by robust government policies, increasing consumer awareness, and technological advancements [1, 4, 5, 6]. India, the world's fifth-largest car market, is poised to become one of the top three, with an estimated 40 crore customers needing mobility solutions by 2030 [2]. The country is striving to reduce its carbon footprint, cut oil imports, and achieve Net Zero Emissions by 2070, making vehicle electrification a critical component of its transportation revolution [2, 5].

The market is projected for substantial growth, with varying estimates for its size and Compound Annual Growth Rate (CAGR). One source indicates an increase of USD 19.58 billion at a CAGR of 18.3% from 2025 to 2030 [1]. Other projections estimate the market size at USD 54.41 billion in 2025, doubling to USD 110.7 billion by 2029 with a CAGR of 19.44% [5]. Another forecast values the market at USD 3.98 billion in 2024, growing to USD 17.88 billion by 2032 at a CAGR of 19.0% [6].

Government initiatives, such as the FAME (Faster Adoption and Manufacturing of Hybrid and Electric Vehicles) schemes, Production-Linked Incentives (PLI), and the PM E-DRIVE program, are central to stimulating demand, supporting domestic manufacturing, and expanding charging infrastructure [1, 4, 5, 6, 7, 9, 11]. While passenger cars accounted for the largest market revenue share in 2024 [1], the market's momentum is primarily fueled by the two- and three-wheeler segments, which dominate last-mile connectivity and urban transport [1, 5, 6]. Despite impressive growth, challenges such as lagging charging infrastructure, price sensitivity among consumers, fiscal policy instability, and an inverted duty structure persist [1, 5, 10]. The market is transitioning from policy-driven adoption to early-stage commercialization, with a strong focus on localizing the supply chain [1, 6].

### 2. Key Findings

*   **Significant Market Growth Projected:** The Indian EV market is projected to grow substantially, with various forecasts indicating a CAGR between 18.3% and 19.44% over different periods [1, 5, 6]. Market size estimates vary, with one source projecting an increase of USD 19.58 billion from 2025-2030 [1], another estimating USD 54.41 billion in 2025 growing to USD 110.7 billion by 2029 [5], and a third valuing it at USD 3.98 billion in 2024, reaching USD 17.88 billion by 2032 [6].
*   **Government as a Primary Catalyst:** Comprehensive government policy frameworks, fiscal incentives, and schemes like FAME I, FAME II, E-Drive, and PLI are the main drivers of market expansion [1, 4, 5, 6, 7, 9, 10, 11]. The government aims for a 30% EV share of total vehicle sales by 2030 [5, 7, 10].
*   **Two- and Three-Wheelers Lead Adoption:** While passenger cars held the largest market revenue share in 2024 [1], the two-wheeler (2W) and three-wheeler (3W) segments are the primary drivers of market momentum and account for the largest volume share due to affordability and suitability for urban commuting and last-mile delivery [1, 5, 6, 11].
*   **Charging Infrastructure Expansion is Critical:** The normal charging segment was valued at USD 10.61 billion in 2024 and is estimated to witness significant growth [1]. As of August 2025, India had approximately 29,300 public charging stations [1], with another source stating over 29,000 public charging stations [6]. The government plans to add approximately 72,000 new EV chargers by FY26, including around 22,100 fast chargers for cars [7].
*   **Challenges Remain:** Key hurdles include lagging charging infrastructure development, high price sensitivity among Indian consumers, fiscal policy instability, and an inverted duty structure that can impede domestic manufacturing [1, 5]. Range anxiety and the need for vehicle modifications for Indian road conditions are also noted [1, 5].
*   **Localization and Domestic Manufacturing Focus:** There is a strong focus on localizing the supply chain through production-linked incentives to reduce dependency on imported components and lower the total cost of ownership [1, 5, 6, 7, 11]. Domestic manufacturers like Tata Motors, MG Motor, and Mahindra dominate the market, with Tata Motors commanding over 53% of the electric passenger vehicle segment [5].
*   **EV Efficiency and Environmental Benefits:** EVs are approximately 3–4 times more efficient than comparable internal combustion engine vehicles (ICEV), offer zero tailpipe emissions, and reduce petroleum dependency, aligning with India's Net Zero Emissions by 2070 goal [2].

### 3. Detailed Analysis

#### 3.1 Market Size and Growth

The Indian Electric Vehicle market is in a high-growth phase, characterized by varying projections across different sources. Technavio estimates the market size to increase by USD 19.58 billion at a Compound Annual Growth Rate (CAGR) of 18.3% from 2025 to 2030 [1]. Cornell University's Business School projects the market size at USD 54.41 billion in 2025, with an expected doubling to USD 110.7 billion by 2029, registering a CAGR of 19.44% [5]. Fortune Business Insights provides a different baseline, valuing the market at USD 3.98 billion in 2024, projected to grow to USD 17.88 billion by 2032, exhibiting a CAGR of 19.0% during the forecast period [6]. These figures consistently indicate robust growth, with CAGR estimates clustering around 18-19% over different forecast horizons.

The market opportunities are valued at USD 24.77 billion, with future opportunities estimated at USD 19.58 billion [1]. Government estimates suggest annual EV sales could reach 17 million units by 2030, with overall EV penetration expected to grow eightfold from current levels [5].

#### 3.2 Market Segmentation

**By Vehicle Type:**
The Indian EV market exhibits a segmented adoption curve. While the passenger car segment accounted for the largest market revenue share in 2024 [1], the market's momentum is primarily fueled by the two- and three-wheeler segments [1, 5, 6]. Two-wheelers (2W) lead in volume share due to affordability and suitability for urban commuting, making up over 50% of annual EV sales [5, 6]. Three-wheelers (3W) follow at 36% of annual EV sales, particularly in logistics and last-mile delivery applications [5, 6]. Passenger electric vehicles, though growing, remain in a scaling phase, constrained by pricing sensitivity and infrastructure limitations [6].

As of January 2026, the overall penetration of EVs in the 2W market was 6.6% [8]. For passenger 3W L5 autos, it was 34.1%, and for cargo 3W L5 autos, it was 22% [8]. For 4W (passenger vehicles), EV penetration was 3.6% [8].

*   **Two-Wheelers (2W):** In January 2026, the 2W market remained largely ICE-driven. Hero MotoCorp had a 2.7% EV share (13,274 units out of 491,684 total sales), and Honda had a 0.1% EV share (269 units out of 472,269). TVS Motor (9.5% EV share with 34,440 EVs out of 363,580) and Bajaj Auto (13.1% EV share with 25,520 EVs out of 195,449) showed stronger EV adoption. Pure EV players like Ather (21,923 units), Ola Electric (7,512 units), and Ampere (5,336 units) reported 100% EV sales [8].
*   **Three-Wheelers (3W):**
    *   **Passenger Auto (L5):** In January 2026, Bajaj Auto led with 38,562 units (20.4% EV share, 7,853 EVs). Mahindra Last Mile Mobility had a strong 95.7% EV share (6,309 EVs out of 6,591 units). TVS Motor reported 47.5% EV share (2,561 EVs out of 5,393 units) [8].
    *   **Cargo (L5):** In January 2026, Bajaj Auto led with 6,225 units (7.5% EV share, 465 EVs). Mahindra Last Mile Mobility had the highest EV share among major OEMs at 50.8% (471 EVs out of 927 units). Pure EV players like Omega Seiki (370 units), Euler Motors (217 units), and Green Evolve (140 units) reported 100% electric sales [8].
*   **Passenger Vehicles (4W):** In January 2026, Maruti Suzuki led the overall market with 214,797 units but had a negligible EV share of 0.1% (210 units). Hyundai had 0.5% EV penetration (326 units out of 65,249). Tata Motors showed the strongest EV volumes among major OEMs with 7,863 EVs out of 62,886 units (12.5%). JSW MG Motor India stood out with the highest EV share at 79.3% (4,606 EVs out of 5,810 units) [8].

**By Charging:**
The normal charging segment, primarily composed of AC chargers, forms the bedrock of the EV market in India, supporting mass adoption of electric two-wheelers and three-wheeler cargo [1]. This segment was valued at USD 10.61 billion in 2024 and is estimated to witness significant growth during the forecast period [1]. Normal charging infrastructure is concentrated in residential and workplace settings, aligning with longer parking durations [1].

**By Type:**
The Indian electric vehicle market comprises all vehicles that run fully or partially on electricity, including Battery Electric Vehicles (BEVs), Plug-in Hybrid Electric Vehicles (PHEVs), and Hybrid Electric Vehicles (HEVs) [2, 6]. BEVs are 100% propelled by electric power, with typical autonomy ranging from 160 to 250 km, and some reaching 500 km on a single charge [2]. PHEVs combine a conventional engine with an electric engine charged externally, capable of significantly reducing fuel consumption [2]. HEVs use both engines but cannot be plugged into the grid, with the battery charged by the combustion engine or regenerative braking [2].

#### 3.3 Drivers of Growth

*   **Government Policies and Incentives:** Comprehensive government policy frameworks and fiscal incentives are the primary drivers [1, 4]. Schemes like FAME (Faster Adoption and Manufacturing of Hybrid and Electric Vehicles), E-Drive, and Production-Linked Incentives (PLI) provide subsidies for EV purchases, support charging infrastructure, and promote domestic manufacturing of automotive components and batteries [1, 4, 5, 6, 7, 9, 11]. The government has also reduced Goods and Services Tax (GST) on EVs from 12% to 5% and offers additional income tax deductions on loans for EV purchases [11].
*   **Technological Advancements:** Innovations in battery technology, leading to enhanced battery efficiency, reduced charging times, and improved energy density and lifespan, are making EVs more appealing [1, 4]. The expansion of high-speed charging networks is also critical for addressing range anxiety [1].
*   **Environmental Concerns and Energy Security:** Vehicle electrification is a game-changer for the transportation sector due to zero tailpipe emissions and reduced petroleum dependency [2]. India's commitment to achieving Net Zero Emissions by 2070 and reducing carbon footprint drives the shift towards EVs [2, 5].
*   **Consumer Awareness and Demand:** A noticeable shift in consumer attitudes, driven by increasing awareness of environmental issues and the benefits of electric mobility (e.g., lower operating costs, reduced emissions), is fostering a favorable environment for EVs [4].
*   **Institutional Adoption:** Fleet operators, ride-hailing platforms, e-commerce logistics providers, and urban delivery networks are accelerating fleet electrification due to predictable usage patterns and operating cost advantages, prioritizing total cost of ownership [1, 6].

#### 3.4 Policy Landscape

India's government has introduced a series of policies to catalyze EV adoption and manufacturing:

*   **FAME (Faster Adoption and Manufacturing of Hybrid and Electric Vehicles) Scheme:**
    *   **FAME I (2015):** Aimed to promote EV adoption through subsidies for electric two-wheelers, three-wheelers, buses, and hybrid vehicles [10, 11].
    *   **FAME II (2019):** Launched in April 2019 with an initial budget of ₹10,000 crore, later increased to ₹11,500 crore (US$ 1.31 billion), concluding in March 2024 [7, 9, 11]. It focused on infrastructure development, incentivizing charging stations, and mandating higher localization requirements [11]. As of June 30, 2025, FAME II supported over 16.29 lakh electric vehicles, including 14.35 lakh e-2 wheelers, 1.65 lakh e-3 wheelers, 22,644 e-4 wheelers, and 5,165 electric buses [7]. Approximately 69% of the funds earmarked under FAME II were utilized [9].
*   **E-Drive Scheme:** Replaced FAME in 2023, with its budget surging by 114% in 2025 to support charging infrastructure and public transport electrification [5]. The Ministry of Heavy Industries launched the PM E-DRIVE Scheme with Rs. 10,900 crore (US$ 1.28 billion) to boost India's EV ecosystem [7]. The EMPS 2024 scheme has been merged into PM E-DRIVE, retaining similar incentives [7].
*   **Production-Linked Incentives (PLIs):** Incentives for domestic manufacturing of automotive components and batteries have jumped over 700%, signaling a strategic push for local value addition [5]. The PLI allocation for automobiles and auto components increased to Rs. 3,500 crores (US$ 409 million) in FY25, up from Rs. 604 crores (US$ 70 million) in FY24 [7]. This scheme aims to boost domestic manufacturing of Advanced Cell Chemistry (ACC) batteries and EV components, reducing import dependence [11].
*   **New EV Policy (2024):** With a USD 500 million outlay, this policy aims to attract global investment, tie incentives to local manufacturing, and provide Indian consumers access to advanced EV models [5].
*   **Charging Infrastructure Support:** The government has sanctioned Rs. 1,062 crore (US$ 120 million) in August 2025 for 85 civil depot and 88 power projects, with Rs. 475 crore (US$ 54 million) already disbursed to nine states/UTs to boost EV accessibility, affordability, and localization [7]. The government also encourages charging stations at 25 km intervals on highways by offering tax rebates [11].
*   **National EV Policy (Proposed):** In August 2025, NITI Aayog proposed a national EV policy focusing on mandates and disincentives rather than subsidies, targeting 30% EV share of total vehicle sales by 2030 and pilot programs for 100% electrification of buses and freight vehicles [7].
*   **State-Level Policies:** State governments play a critical role by creating tailored policies including demand-side incentives, R&D support, labor incentives, and infrastructure development. States like Karnataka, Maharashtra, and Tamil Nadu lead in EV registrations and have introduced comprehensive policies [11]. For example, in June 2025, the Tamil Nadu State Planning Commission unveiled a roadmap to position the state as a global EV innovation hub [7].

### 4. Challenges & Risks

*   **Infrastructure Limitations:** Despite rapid expansion, charging infrastructure development lags behind demand, especially outside major urban centers [5]. As of August 2025, approximately 29,300 public charging stations were deployed [1], while another source states over 29,000 public charging stations [6]. However, the number of public charging stations installed out of those sanctioned under FAME II was 8,885 out of 9,332 as of June 30, 2025 [7]. This indicates a potential gap between sanctioned and actual deployment, or different reporting methodologies. The lack of high-speed charging networks contributes to range anxiety [1].
*   **Cost and Affordability:** Most Indian consumers are highly price-conscious [5]. While domestic EVs are priced well below USD 20,000, imported models can cost double [5]. The initial acquisition cost is a significant barrier, requiring fleet operators to analyze long-term savings from lower fuel and maintenance expenses, factoring in subsidies [1]. Passenger electric vehicles remain constrained by pricing sensitivity [6].
*   **Policy Instability and Duty Structure:** The industry grapples with challenges like fiscal policy instability and an inverted duty structure that can impede the progress of domestic manufacturing [1]. While central subsidies have boosted sales, their effectiveness differs across segments, and purchase subsidies alone may not transform overall market composition without complementary measures [10].
*   **Technological and Adaptability:** Range anxiety remains a concern, necessitating innovations in battery technology and the expansion of charging networks [1]. Indian road conditions and consumer preferences require modifications to global EV models, such as increased ground clearance and durability enhancements [5].
*   **Market Penetration Gaps:** While the electric two-wheeler market saw a significant boost in sales during FAME-II (2019-2023), the adoption rate (percentage of E2W sales compared to total two-wheeler sales) did not increase noticeably, suggesting purchase subsidies alone are insufficient for market transformation [10]. Similarly, passenger vehicles remain in a scaling phase due to pricing sensitivity and infrastructure limitations [6].

### 5. Future Outlook

The Indian EV market is poised for substantial expansion, reflecting a broader commitment to sustainable transportation solutions and a structural shift in mobility demand [4, 6]. The government's ambitious target of 30% EV share of total vehicle sales by 2030, coupled with ongoing policy support and investment, will continue to drive growth [5, 7, 10]. The market is transitioning from policy-driven adoption towards early-stage commercialization, supported by regulatory alignment, urban mobility demand, and gradual cost normalization [6].

The focus on localizing the supply chain through Production-Linked Incentives (PLI) is expected to reduce import dependency and lower the total cost of ownership, making EVs more accessible [1, 5, 6]. Continued advancements in battery technology and the expansion of charging infrastructure, including the government's plan to add approximately 72,000 new EV chargers by FY26, are critical for addressing range anxiety and ensuring long-term viability [1, 4, 7].

The two- and three-wheeler segments are expected to continue leading the charge, especially in last-mile connectivity and urban transport [1, 5, 6]. Increasing electrification in commercial fleets is also likely to broaden market depth and accelerate overall penetration [6]. The imminent entry of global players like Tesla, reportedly planning a USD 2 billion investment, is a major development that could further stimulate competition and innovation in the market [5].

The government's long-term vision includes ambitious plans such as transitioning 800,000 diesel buses to environmentally friendly alternatives within seven years, which would significantly diminish vehicular emissions and stimulate investments in national EV infrastructure [7]. Overall, India's EV ecosystem is rapidly diversifying across propulsion types, vehicle classes, and components, indicating a maturation of the value chain and positioning India as a key emerging EV market globally [6].
